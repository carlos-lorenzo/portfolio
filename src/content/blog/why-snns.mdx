As you might have already guessed, these neural networks don't really replicate the brain's structure in the way your thought.

If we make the analogy between the LLM's "neurons" with your biological neurons, the articial neurons are constantly processing information whilst your smart neurons process information sparsely, only when its needed. 
This is know as a neuron "spiking" or "firing".

### The Leaky Integrate-and-Fire Model


We've already mentioned how biological neurons spike sparsely. They spike (roughly) based on weather or not the neurons its synapticly connected to also spike. 
Turns out that a neuron can decide how much importance to give to the firing of each of its synaptic neurons. This is known as **synaptic weights**. This is exactly what neural networks have copied!
Not the neurons themselves as we will now see but rather the idea of having a bunch of entities related to eachother by a numeric weight determining its importance.

Achieving brain-like behaviour (and efficiency) is as "simple" as replacing traditional neurons with their weight tensor, bias and activation function with a tensor of (synaptic) weights and a simulation of biological neuron.
A spiking neural network (SNN) is simply a traditional neural network where the articial neurons have been replaced by neurons that replicate real neuronal spiking, hence "spiking".

Luckily, this model has been figured out for decades thanks to the work done by Hodgkin and Huxley. Through a collection of 4 linear ordinary differential equations (ODEs), the effect that input currents (other neurons outputs) have
on the spiking of a neuron is perfectly captured. Without going into too much detail the neuron has an internal "counter" called the **membrane potential**, once this potential reaches a certain threshold (depolarisation) the neuron spikes and resets (repolarisation). 
If you want to learn more about the Hodgkin-Huxley model you can read about it [here](/blog/hh-model). 

Turns out however that simulating the HH model is extremely expensive and completly unusable in practice. However we only truly care about the spiking resembling a real neuron, we don't care about the exact vaues of the potential. This is where 
the Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) model comes into play. It's the foundation of many SNN architectures as it elegantly mimics the membrane potential $V(t)$ charging over time of a biological neuron. The dynamics are governed instead by a **single** first-order differential equation:


---
$$
\tau_m \frac{dV}{dt} = -[V(t) - E_L] + R_m I(t)
$$

Where:
- $\tau_m$ is the membrane time constant.
- $E_L$ is the resting potential of the neuron.
- $R_m$ represents the membrane resistance.
- $I(t)$ is the incoming current from pre-synaptic neurons.

---


Below is a live visualization of the LIF ODE. You can play with the constant current $I(t)$ and see how it reacts.

Notice how if the current is sub-threshold ($I < 1.0$), the passive membrane decay outpaces the accumulation. The neuron remains in a resting state. Once the input current drives the potential to the voltage threshold, 
the neuron fires an action potential (spike), followed by a hyperpolarization reset and a brief refractory period. In fact, the $-[V(t) - E_L]$ acts as a low pass filter, crucial in DSP.

## Discrepancies

Consider a prosthetic hand. A traditional ANN processes every pixel or EMG channel at a fixed frequency (e.g., 60Hz), wasting cycles on 'silence.' 
An SNN-based controller only 'wakes up' when a sensor detects a change in pressure or a motor spike. 
This is the difference between a system that is Always-On and a system that is **Event Driven**. The difference between your brain and ANNs.

They however aren't perfect.

## Reality Check

If this is so fantastic why isn't it being implemented everywhere? Turns out that simulating even the single ODE is expensive over a whole network. Artificial neural networks have evolved to squeeze the maximum performance out of our computer architectures 
through the extensive use of the GPU's incredibly fast tensor operation capabilities. And are in fact great at certain tasks like numeric computations or image processing.

Standard GPU architectures suffer from the **Von Neumann bottleneck**: energy expensive transfer of data between memory and the processor. 
We're missing hardware capable of efficiently replicating these neuronal dynamics: when a current is ran through it, it will spike and behave exactly the simulation. 
This hardware known as **Neuromorphic** and implements **In-Memory** computing, where the 'synapse' (weight) and the 'neuron' (processor) are physically co-located which enables massively parallel, event-driven computation.
Neuromorphic hardware like Intel’s **Loihi** or IBM’s **NorthPole** is starting to be released but its still far from being usable at scale. This area is in fact at the very forefront of research.
